# Amanda's Writing Voice — Global

Applies to: **any user-facing prose written in Amanda's name.** Portfolio
copy, project READMEs, project descriptions, essays, cover letters,
LinkedIn posts, technical docs with a human register, email drafts,
blog posts, PR descriptions, changelog entries.

Not for: automated system messages, code comments, log strings, or
purely mechanical UI microcopy (labels, error toasts).

Complements — does not replace — `portfolio-writing.md` (structural
rules for data-journalism pieces). When both apply, this one governs
the voice; the other governs the structure.

Read this file before drafting or heavily editing any prose in
Amanda's name.

---

## What Amanda's voice actually sounds like

Three anchor examples pulled from her own work.

**Portfolio site opener (amandarae.dev):**
> "Hello! My name is Amanda. I build things on the world wide web."

**Portfolio project description (retirement calculator):**
> "A retirement calculator built to replace the overwhelming,
> question-heavy tools that make you want to close the tab. Clean
> inputs, a chart that means something, and insights that show what
> your money is quietly working toward."

**Where It Counts closer:**
> "A meaningful shift in a state-legislative district's math takes
> thousands of movers, not hundreds. That's the honest scale of the
> ask. This is a long-form civic move, not a stunt."

Shared DNA across all three:

- **Warm without being cute** — welcoming but not performing
- **Direct without being cold** — vernacular, not clinical
- **Uses actual human phrases** — "makes you want to close the tab,"
  "means something," "the honest scale of the ask"
- **Never posturing, never selling** — states the thing, trusts the
  reader to see why it matters

If a draft doesn't sound like the same person wrote those three,
rewrite it.

## Rules

### 1. Human phrases over jargon

Prefer language a smart friend would use over language a consultant
would use.

- ✅ "makes you want to close the tab"
- ✅ "means something"
- ✅ "quietly working toward"
- ✅ "honest scale of the ask"
- ❌ "leverages best-in-class UX to drive engagement"
- ❌ "utilizes cutting-edge technologies"
- ❌ "empowers users to achieve their goals"

### 2. Numbers and specifics carry the load

When you can say the actual number, say it. When you can name the
actual thing, name it.

- ✅ "216 times Georgia's decisive margin"
- ✅ "Tableau → D3.js → Angular"
- ✅ "$1,891/month saved — nearly $23,000 a year"
- ❌ "significantly larger"
- ❌ "used modern tools"
- ❌ "substantial cost savings"

The test: if a sentence would survive with the specific number
removed, remove the sentence. If it wouldn't, keep the number
front and center.

### 3. Terse tech-stack callouts

Never brag about the stack. State it. Move on.

- ✅ "D3.js & Vanilla JS"
- ✅ "React, Typescript, Storybook, Git, Npm"
- ✅ "Angular, Typescript"
- ✅ "Tableau → D3.js → Angular" (arrow shows progression, still terse)
- ❌ "This project showcases the powerful capabilities of..."
- ❌ "Built with a modern, scalable, cutting-edge stack including..."

### 4. Focus on the reader's problem, not your cleverness

Project descriptions live or die on whether the reader can see
themselves as the user. Frame around what the tool does *for a
person*, not what it demonstrates about the developer.

- ✅ "built to replace the overwhelming, question-heavy tools that
  make you want to close the tab"
- ✅ "The resume reimagined. This aims to intuitively answer the
  most common resume questions that result from gaps in the
  traditional resume."
- ❌ "features an intuitive UI and modern architecture"
- ❌ "demonstrates my proficiency in..."

### 5. Self-aware humor, used sparingly

Occasional gentle humor makes writing feel human. Overusing it
makes it feel forced.

- ✅ "This website... duh" (used once, as the description for the
  portfolio site pointing at itself)
- ✅ Ellipses as a conversational gesture, not filler
- ❌ Meme voice throughout
- ❌ Constant winking at the reader
- ❌ Emoji as substitute for content

### 6. First-person is fine — make it count

Amanda's voice is comfortable in first person. Use it when it adds
warmth or signals the human at the keyboard. Skip it when it would
come off as bragging.

- ✅ "My name is Amanda. I build things on the world wide web."
- ✅ "My consulting journey through various industries has
  equipped me with a broad toolkit..."
- ✅ "I'm a front-end developer who thrives at the intersection of
  the highly technical and the creative."
- ❌ "I designed a beautiful..."
- ❌ "I successfully implemented..."
- ❌ "I'm passionate about..."

### 7. Warm greetings, tight signoffs

Openers should feel like the door opening. Closers should feel like
the handshake, not the pitch.

- ✅ "Hello!"
- ✅ "Let's Connect"
- ✅ "You are here" (as a signoff button on the current site)
- ❌ "Greetings, esteemed visitor"
- ❌ "Thanks for stopping by! Don't forget to like and subscribe"

### 8. Own the frame

When a comparison or claim is illustrative rather than causal,
name it in the same sentence.

- ✅ "California's surplus was 216 times Georgia's decisive margin.
  Those votes couldn't cross state lines — that's precisely what
  makes geography so decisive."
- ❌ "California's surplus could have flipped Georgia 216 times over"
  (implies mechanically possible causation that isn't real)

The extra sentence acknowledging the framing costs almost nothing
and buys the writing's credibility.

### 9. No apologies, no preamble

Show the thing. Explain only when explanation adds value. Never
apologize for existing.

- ✅ "The resume reimagined."
- ✅ "An interactive sudoku puzzle built with React and Typescript."
- ❌ "I hope you enjoy this small project I've been working on..."
- ❌ "In this post, I'll be discussing..."
- ❌ "Sorry for the delay, but here's..."

### 10. Consistency across surfaces

The voice on the portfolio should be recognizable in the README,
in a project description, in the LinkedIn caption, in the follow-up
email. Different *registers* are fine (blog post is longer than a
button label), but the *voice* is one voice.

If someone read all four side by side, they should think "same
person, same values, same care" — not "this looks like the
portfolio was written by someone else."

### 11. Short paragraphs, active verbs

Amanda's writing breathes. Avoid the wall-of-text. Every paragraph
earns its own line of thought.

- ✅ Multiple short paragraphs, each with one idea
- ✅ Active verbs ("build," "reimagined," "shows what your money is
  working toward")
- ❌ One long paragraph with three ideas
- ❌ Passive voice ("was designed to be a tool that could...")

### 12. When you list things, list them like a person

Comma-separated inline for short lists. Actual bullets when
they help the reader scan. Never a list where a sentence works.

- ✅ "Clean inputs, a chart that means something, and insights..."
- ✅ Actual bullets for feature lists in a README
- ❌ Bullets for a two-item list
- ❌ Bullets when the ideas want to flow

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- **Résumé speak** — "leveraged," "spearheaded," "utilized,"
  "orchestrated," "cutting-edge," "world-class," "robust,"
  "innovative solutions"
- **Corporate softeners** — "at [company], we believe...", "our
  commitment to excellence," "delighting customers"
- **Passive stack listing** — "the project makes use of React"
- **Manufactured enthusiasm** — "Excited to share!!! 🚀" for
  everything
- **Under-owned rhetorical claims** — comparing incomparables
  without acknowledging it
- **Preamble** — "In this article, I'll be discussing..."
- **Signposting** — "Now let's turn to..." "As mentioned above..."
- **Meta-commentary about the writing itself** — "As you can see
  from the above..."
- **Sales-tone urgency** — "Don't miss out!" "Get started today!"

## When to bend the rules

- **Formal grant / contract writing** — grant applications, federal
  proposals, academic submissions, security review documentation.
  Same voice, less colloquial. Keep the directness; drop the
  vernacular.
- **Highly technical READMEs for infra tools** — less "close the
  tab," more precise. Still no jargon padding, still no résumé
  verbs.
- **Legal / privacy pages** — plain and clear, not conversational.
  These are pages where the reader wants the letter of the thing,
  not the voice of the writer.
- **Ghost-writing for a company or client** — the voice of the
  client dominates. This skill applies only when the writing is in
  Amanda's own name.
- **When Amanda has explicitly asked for a different register** —
  she's the boss. Her tone directive wins over these rules.

## Quick self-check before shipping

Read the draft aloud. If it sounds like any of these, rewrite:

- 🚫 A consultant selling a deck
- 🚫 A LinkedIn thought-leader post
- 🚫 Marketing copy trying to inspire
- 🚫 A résumé's "professional summary"
- 🚫 ChatGPT with the settings turned up

If it sounds like any of these, keep:

- ✅ A smart friend explaining something they care about
- ✅ Someone who trusts the reader to be intelligent
- ✅ A person who has done the work and can describe it plainly
- ✅ A teacher who respects the student
